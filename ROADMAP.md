# DSCraft Expansion Roadmap

This roadmap prioritizes the 34 planning issues (#7–#38, #40, #41) filed against `gr3enarr0w/dscraft` from a two-machine DS/ML tooling audit, a review of the open textbook *Forecasting: Principles and Practice, the Pythonic Way* (Hyndman et al., OTexts.com/fpppy), and the architecture doc's own post-v1 MCP/Skill direction. Every issue is planning-only — none of this has been implemented yet. This document exists to hand off a defensible execution order, not to guess at one.

## Methodology: RICED (RICE + Dependency)

Each issue is scored with **RICED**: `(Reach × Impact × Confidence × Dependency) ÷ Effort`. This starts from the standard **RICE** framework (Reach × Impact × Confidence ÷ Effort, used at Intercom and widely elsewhere) — chosen specifically to avoid ranking-by-gut-feel — and adds a **Dependency** factor, because plain RICE has no mechanism for the fact that some issues unlock or de-risk others, and some issues are themselves gated by another issue landing first. Without it, foundational/enabling work (e.g. adopting a shared metrics library before four other issues would each reinvent it) scores no differently than an unrelated standalone issue of the same size — which is wrong, and was caught and corrected during this roadmap's own review.

- **Reach (1–5)**: how many independent real evidence sources (real local projects, textbook chapters, or other filed issues that depend on this landing first) support this issue. 5 = cross-cutting/affects most of the roadmap; 1 = single evidence source.
- **Impact (0.5–5)**: value delivered where it lands. 5 = flagship, currently-nonexistent capability a whole subpackage's stated purpose depends on; 3 = high; 2 = medium; 1 = low; 0.5 = minimal (e.g. cosmetic). Note: an issue's *standalone* impact — without its dependencies satisfied — is scored here, not its potential impact once combined with something else (see Dependency below for how that's handled instead). **Impact is scored against the whole platform's likely usage, not just centrality within one subpackage** — a capability that's "flagship" *inside* a narrow-audience subpackage (e.g. `agent`'s LLM/RAG-dependent tooling, only relevant to users doing agent/RAG work) does not automatically score Impact=5 the way a comparably-central capability in a broadly-applicable subpackage does (e.g. general tabular/EDA capabilities useful to every DS/ML user regardless of whether they touch LLMs at all). This was corrected on 2026-07-21 after #12/#13 were initially scored Impact=5 purely on subpackage-centrality — see the "Correction" note below the ranked table.
- **Confidence (50%/65%/80%/100%)**: how solid the Reach/Impact estimate is. 100% = real, repeated, or currently-active evidence with no open technical risk. 80% = solid but narrower evidence. 65% = single-source evidence or genuine open technical risk (e.g. unverified MPS support, a build-toolchain dependency, an unresolved architectural trigger condition). 50% = speculative (not used below — everything here has at least single-source real evidence).
- **Effort (0.5/1/2/3/4/6, relative units)**: 0.5 = pure evaluation/decision (no implementation), 1 = small self-contained PR, 2 = medium (new dependency + real integration work), 3 = medium-large, 4 = large (new subsystem), 6 = very large.
- **Dependency (0.5/0.75/1.0/1.5/2.0)**: how this issue's dependency relationships with *other filed issues* should move its priority.
  - **2.0** — unblocks or de-risks 3+ other filed issues (strong enabler).
  - **1.5** — unblocks or de-risks 1–2 other issues.
  - **1.0** — neutral; no meaningful dependency relationship in either direction.
  - **0.75** — mildly gated by another unimplemented issue (partial standalone value still exists).
  - **0.5** — substantially blocked; most of this issue's real value depends on another issue landing first.

### A known, deliberate limitation — read before treating this as a strict sequence

Dividing by Effort still means RICED favors small, low-risk wins over large bets. That's a real, permanent property of this framework — it isn't corrected for elsewhere in this document, and it isn't a signal that large-effort issues are unimportant; it's a signal to sanity-check the ranking against context (parallel tracks, team capacity) rather than follow it mechanically when an issue's size alone pushes it down.

### Correction, 2026-07-21: Impact was initially over-weighted toward subpackage-centrality

#12/#13 (`agent`'s RAG pipeline and benchmark-adapter layer) were originally scored Impact=5 on the reasoning that they're the flagship capability `dscraft.agent`'s stated purpose depends on. That reasoning conflated "central to its own subpackage" with "high value across the whole platform" — LLM/RAG/agent-benchmark tooling only matters to users already doing agent/RAG work, a narrower slice of DSCraft's overall audience than general-purpose tabular/EDA/recommender capabilities that apply regardless of whether someone touches LLMs at all. Corrected: #12/#13's Impact dropped 5→3 (RICED 6.25→3.75 each), and #15/#16 (EDA: projection, warehouse connectors) and #32 (recommender-systems evaluation) — capabilities with genuinely broad, LLM-independent applicability that had been scored as merely "medium" Impact (2) — were raised to Impact=3, reflecting that same broader-applicability weighting (RICED: #15/#16 1.30→1.95, #32 2.60→3.90). No other issue's Impact was touched; this correction applies specifically to the "narrow-but-central vs. broad-but-generic" distinction, not a general re-inflation.

## How to use this on handoff

1. Work the table top-down as the default sequence — it's the objective, evidence-derived order, not a suggestion to second-guess per-issue.
2. Note the cluster at ranks 2–5: #29, #38, #14 (all enablers) and #22 (feeds #23/#20) land immediately after the one free quick win (#20) specifically *because* they're foundational — do these before touching the individual capability-addition issues they de-risk.
3. #12/#13 (`agent`) now rank where their evidence and platform-wide impact actually place them (see the 2026-07-21 correction note above) — there is no standing recommendation to jump them ahead of their rank. If a real dependency or capacity reason to parallelize them emerges later, make that case explicitly rather than assuming it from their being large-effort.
4. #40 and #41 are a linked pair (MCP server, then a Skill that calls it) — #40 ranks above #41 precisely because #41's real value depends on #40 existing. If #40 is deprioritized or descoped, re-score #41 rather than treating its current score as fixed.
5. Every issue is independently scoped and cites its real evidence in its GitHub issue body — read the issue itself before starting, not just this table.
6. Ties (same RICED score) have no implied order between them; sequence by whichever fits current context (e.g. don't context-switch dependency stacks mid-batch).
7. This ranking reflects evidence gathered on 2026-07-20 from two machines' `~/Development` folders, the *fpppy* textbook, and the architecture doc's own stated post-v1 direction. If new real usage or new dependency relationships are found later, re-score rather than append unscored issues to the bottom.
